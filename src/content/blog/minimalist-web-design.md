Minimalist design is not about removing things until nothing is left. It is about removing things until only what matters remains. The distinction matters: the first produces empty screens, the second produces calm ones.

## Constraints force decisions

When I built this site, I gave myself three constraints:

1. One typeface family.
2. One accent color.
3. No animations longer than 200ms.

Every decision I made afterward was forced by these constraints. The header cannot use three weights because I only have one. The terminal cannot have a rainbow palette because there is no palette to pull from. Constraints are liberating.

## Hierarchy comes from spacing

Without color to lean on, hierarchy has to come from somewhere. For me that meant generous vertical rhythm — large titles, generous line-height, and consistent padding. The eye learns where to look because each section's shape is the same.

## Refuse ornament

A underline can be a link. A bold weight can be a heading. A border can be a card. You do not need arrows, icons, or color shifts for the user to understand what is clickable. The web has spent twenty years adding chrome; it is fine to give some of it back.

## The test

A minimalist design is good when a stranger can tell what to do within two seconds, and a returning visitor does not notice the design at all. If either fails, you removed something you shouldn't have — or kept something you shouldn't.
